ASML

Test Engineer

ASML is a Dutch company which in laymans terms makes the machines that make microchips. Specifically, they make the machine that performs the most detailed part of the process, semiconductor lithography.

While a test engineer at ASML, my main duties involved keeping a lab running and assisting other engineers construct/deconstruct their test setups and then assisting with the execution of those tests. This could involve delicate vacuum systems, Helium leak checking, heater and thermocouple placement, pneumatic valve operation, microscopy etc.

My main passion project, which led me to journalism though, was advocating for fiscally pragmatic and sustainable 401(k) fund options by drafting petition letters and slide decks for tailored pitches to management. These pitch decks were vigorously researched from hundreds of pages of shareholder materials and other approved statements from management to synthesize cohesive arguments for concrete action to lobby in support of the SEC's campaign to end subjective climate disclosures and the false-advertising of sustainable investment funds, a practice known as greenwashing.

I also researched and advocated for more investment transparency and better health insurance benefits.

September 2021-June 2025 San Diego, California

Smith & Nephew

R&D Co-op, Knee Repair Division

Smith and Nephew is a global medical technology company. I worked in R&D for surgical devices on the Knee team.

I worked as an R & D Co-op on the Knee Repair team at Smith & Nephew. My primary project revolved around the design of a special new drill that facilitates knee ligament reconstruction (i.e. ACL, PCL, small size meniscal root repairs, etc.). As part of a cross functional team, I generated new parts and assemblies, iterated upon them, worked with machinists to produce prototypes, and tested those prototypes. In addition to these efforts, in order to streamline snapfit design and spring selection, I created semi-automated Microsoft Excel spreadsheets which calculated relevant properties and values and assisted in utilization of these components. This was accomplished using numerous logic commands, mathematical models, and Excel's VBA editor. While on this co-op, I gained experience in test protocol and technical report drafting, as well as executing the tests themselves.

Pivotal Challenges

Principled Hiatus

The three years between graduating from Northeastern and landing my first full-time engineering job I deliberately refused to apply to industries which employ large numbers of entry level mechanical engineers that conflict with my personal and professional ethics.


While consistently applying for more competitive engineering jobs and volunteering for Engineers Without Borders, I supported myself as a restaurant server, a busser, a food-runner, an academic coach, and a math tutor.

Result: I sleep well at night.

Journalism Transition

Employed with a full-time engineering position in San Diego, I found myself unhappy with where my effort was going day-to-day. In my spare time, I took on projects that had nothing to do with engineering with large research and communication components. I was way more productive and happy working on these projects than my job's actual duties.


It took 3 years, plenty of soul searching, and much planning, which led me to move back to the east coast and begin a Journalism program with no prior experience in the humanties.

Result: I've never worked harder or been happier.

Mindset Diversity

Someone with a different background can be useful in developing new sources, picking out otherwise overlooked details, and perhaps broadening the appeal of certain stories.

STEM Schooling

Coming from a completely different discipline means I was trained in a methodology of problem solving uncommon to life-long journalists. I learned among other things calculus, fluid mechanics, thermodynamics, measurement science, and control systems. Whether I held onto the salient details of those courses or would even use the Thermal Resistance Circuits model in my day-to-day life is immaterial. The point is that all those things both taught me how to learn and form the basis for how I approach challenges today.

Perfect Example: Coding Languages
Throughout school I learned the basics of Ruby, Python, C, VBA and Matlab. Despite the fact that today I can't write in any of those languages, learning them taught me to be deliberate and methodical with coding, which has proven to be a solid underground foundation on which to begin learning R, HTML, and CSS.

People Skills & Relatability

Once you've been a Pedicab tour guide, dependent on tips, you develop people skills. For that job you need to talk to strangers to generate business, and be pleasant while you spend the day in the DC summer humidity. I've further developed those skills working in restaurants. As far as exposure to people in different fields, I've been a bike mechanic, academic coach, camp counselor, construction worker, and pseudo assistant school teacher on zoom.

Perfect Example: Department Collaboration
My last engineering position was at a company with a complex organizational structure and occasionally a lot of siloed expertise and resources. The ease with which I cultivated relationships became uniquely useful for quickly solving problems for my team by finding what and who we needed.

Business Minor

At the time, I thought a business minor would be a good way to give myself the tools to start my own company if I invented something cool. We all make wrong turns, because turns out I wanted the brand new Engineering Entrepreneurship minor.
Best. Mistake. Ever.

Perfect Example: Interest in Business Reporting
Especially learning the fundamentals of accounting, but also organizational behavior, and marketing have only become more useful as I have pivoted to journalism.
