Pivotal Challenges

Principled Hiatus

The three years between graduating from Northeastern and landing my first full-time engineering job I deliberately refused to apply to industries that conflicted with my personal and professional ethics, even though they employed a large percentage of entry level mechanical engineers.

While consistently applying for more competitive engineering jobs and volunteering for Engineers Without Borders, I supported myself as a restaurant server, a busser, a food-runner, an academic coach, and a math tutor.

This easy decision was the hardest I've ever had to stand by, but by far the most worth it.

Journalism Transition

Employed with a full-time engineering position in San Diego, I found myself unhappy with where my effort was going day-to-day. In my spare time, I took on projects that had nothing to do with engineering with large research and communication components. I was way more productive and happy working on these projects than my job's actual duties.

After four years, plenty of soul searching, and a great deal of planning, I moved back to the east coast to begin a journalism program with no prior experience in humanties.

I've never worked harder or been happier.

Mindset Diversity

Someone with a different educational background can be useful in developing new sources, picking out otherwise overlooked details, and perhaps broadening the appeal of certain stories.

STEM Schooling

Coming from a completely different discipline means I was trained in a methodology of problem solving uncommon to life-long journalists. Among other subjects, I learned calculus, fluid mechanics, thermodynamics, measurement science, and control systems. Whether I held onto the salient details of those courses or would ever use the Thermal Resistance Circuits model in my day-to-day life is immaterial. The point is that all those concepts taught me how to learn and now form the basis for how I approach challenges today.

Perfect Example:
Coding Languages
 Throughout school I learned the basics of Ruby, Python, C, VBA and Matlab. Despite the fact that today I can't write in any of those languages, learning them taught me to be deliberate and methodical with coding. This has proven to be a solid foundation on which to learn R, HTML, and CSS.

People Skills Relatability

Once you've been a Pedicab tour guide, dependent on tips, you develop people skills. For that job you need to talk to strangers to generate business, and be pleasant while you spend the day in the DC summer humidity. I've further developed those same skills working in restaurants. As far as exposure to people in different fields, I've been a bike mechanic, academic coach, camp counselor, construction worker, and pseudo assistant school teacher.

Perfect Example:
Department Collaboration
My last engineering position was at a company with a complex organizational structure and occasionally a lot of siloed expertise and resources. The ease with which I cultivated relationships became uniquely useful for quickly solving problems for my team by finding what and who we needed.

Business Minor

As a 19-year-old, I thought a business minor would be a good way to give myself the tools to start my own company if I invented something cool. We all make wrong turns, because turns out I wanted the Engineering Entrepreneurship minor.
Best. Mistake. Ever.
