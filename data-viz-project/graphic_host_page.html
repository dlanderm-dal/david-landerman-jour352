Article Summary: The Price of Remission

How a company that didn't invent a drug and didn't discover its life saving potential raised the price of a daily 25 cent pill to cost $325,590 per year to cancer patients who would die without it.



In 2023 David Armstrong, a healthcare reporter for Propublica was diagnosed with Multiple Myeloma, a kind of blood cancer which painfully eats away at the patient's bones and used to have a life expectancy of 2 years. Armstrong was surprised to find first that the ground breaking treatment that challenged this former status quo was Thalidomide, an infamous drug from the 1950s which was prescribed to pregnant women only to cause disastrous birth defects for thousands of children. Secondly, Armstrong was surprised by how much this daily medication, discovered in 1953, could still be so expensive, costing $892/pill.



What followed was an investigation into Celgene, the maker of Revlimid, the reformulated thalidomide and how they exploited loopholes and skirted the law to keep the price of thalidomide high and rake in billions in profits.
