1950RevlimidHow a company that didn't invent a drug and didn't discover its life saving potential raised the price of a daily 25 cent pill to cost $325,590 per year to cancer patients who would die without it.title
3
19531119691230Thalidomide and Birth DefectsThalidomide was discovered and offered in Europe to treat nausea in pregnant women but resulted in severe birth defects for thousands of children. It was later banned worldwide.#333333
4
199211Celgene acquires ThalidomideCelgene acquires Thalidomide patents held by researchers at Rockefeller University
5
1994Dr. Robert D'AmatoDr. Judah Folkman theorized that cancer could be impeded by cutting off its supply of new blood vessels. Dr. Robert D'Amato working under Folkman, thought of thalidomide to deliberately inhibit blood vessel growth. He first tested thalidomide then metabolites of thalidomide which then worked on inhibiting cell growth in experiments he conducted on eggs.
6
199712Beth WolmerBeth Wolmer, a high powered lawyer searching for a way to keep her husband Ira alive after he was diagnosed with Multiple Myeloma in 1995, finds Dr. Folkman and convinces her husband's doctor, Dr. Bart Barlogie, to trial thalidomide to treat her husband who is out of options. Barlogie administers thalidomide to three patients including Wolmer. Ira Wolmer dies a couple months later but one of the other recipients, Jimmy (only first name known) sees his cancer markers decrease.
7
19987Thalomid narrowly approvedCelgene, while in dyer financial straits, even debating whether or not to charge their employees for coffee in the office, receives approval of thalidomide for the treatment of a painful complication of leprosy. A use already in practice in parts of the world for a small group of patients. The brand name for this drug was called Thalomid.
8
199812Wider experiment results revealedArkansas doctors (Barlogie, and Dr. Seema Singhal, Jimmy's doctor) share their initial findings of a larger experiment at annual meeting of American Society of Hematology. Out of 89 patients who were high-risk, had undergone prior treatment, and were out of options, one third had declines in myeloma activity.
9
199912Celgene Profits from ThalomidCelgene sells Thalomid (their first thalidomide drug with a relatively weak patent due to the long history of the drug) for $7.50/pill and increase their revenue sevenfold.
10
20021Celgene and D'AmatoCelgene and EntreMed sue each other and settle. Celgene was trying to find an analog of thalidomide so that it could replace its weak patents with stronger, more exclusive ones. EntreMed Inc was a competitor of Celgene, working with Dr. D'Amato, to also try to find an analog, while patenting those uses. The companies sued each other, then settled. Celgene agreed to pay Boston Childrens Hospital (D'Amato's hospital) royalties from future sales of Revlimid. In exchange, the hospital and DAmato licensed their patents of thalidomide analogs to Celgene. Celgene also agreed to pay EntreMed $27 million.
11
2004Thalomid Price HikeThalomid is sold for $47/pill. An increase of 526% from 1999. Two days later Celgene CEO John Jackson told investors that they had kept the price of Thalomid low when it was initially intended for AIDS patients, as the company didnt want huge numbers of people demonstrating in front of its office. That wasnt a problem with cancer patients. There was plenty of room for very substantial increases in the price of the drug now, Jackson told investors.
12
20056Revlimid ApprovedCelgene launches Revlimid, a thalidomide analog with minor chemical differences to the original. It is given 7 year market exclusivity by the FDA. Celgene charges $280/pill. This represents a 496% price increase from Thalomid's price the previous year. David Schmidt, former national account manager at Celgene, terminated in 2008. Afterwards filed a wrongful termination suit which was voluntarily dismissed. In the filing "There was a common internal theme at Celgene that cancer patients were willing to pay almost any amount Celgene charged.
13
2012Impeding Generics by cutting off access to Brand NameSix generic makers had requested to purchase Revlimid for testing. In every case Celgene refused. All generics have to be tested against the name brand drug. This is due to a safety program mandated by the FDA because of the risk of birth defects. Without access to the name brand drug, the generics could not be tested, could not be approved, and couldnt go to market to challenge the price of Revlimid. Celgene was aware of this and noted it in an internal company presentation.
14
20132After clashes with FDA and FTC, FTC threatens Celgene with legal actionRichard Feinstein of the FTC writes letter to Celgene. We have reached a point where the staff may be instructed in the very near future to commence litigation. Celgene says it will sell to competitors once FDA approves its safety plan. This cools the interest of the commissioners according to Jon Leibowitz, former chairman of the FTC.
15
20137Celgene still refuses to sellFDA approves safety protocols of generic maker Mylan. Celgene then still refuses to sell supply of Revlimid to Mylan for testing
16
201412More Revlimid price hikesCelgene hikes Revlimid 3 times in one year to $469/pill. In 2015 Francis Brown, former sales executive at Celgene in a deposition "They could raise their price any time they wanted to.
17
2015Lawsuit with NATCO PharmaCelgene settles lawsuit brought by generic maker NATCO Pharma. NATCO could bring a generic to market, Celgene agreed, but not for seven more years in March 2022. Even then, the generic would be limited to less than 10% of the total market for Revlimid in the first year, with gradual increases after that. After the deal with NATCO, Celgene's stock jumps 10% the day after its announced. Celgene adopts the same strategy with other generic developers
18
201710Celgene continues to use Revlimid to pad its lossesCelgene announces plans to abandon a drug for Crohn's disease. The same day as this announcement they quietly raised the price of Revlimid 9%
19
201712Revlimid largest cost increase in drugs historyRevlimid stands at $662/pill, a cumulative 20% increase from the previous year. The largest one year increase in the drugs history.
Revlimid is the most expensive Medicare drug of that year, costing the program $3.3 billion to provide it to 37,459 patients.
Over the 12 years after Revlimid was approved, Celgene pays handful of top executives about a half-billion dollars.
Robert Hugin, Celgene's CEO and then Executive Chairman received $51 million in total compensation from 2015-2017.
FTC closes its case in 2017 without taking any action
20
2019Sale to BMSCelgene announces they will be acquired by Bristol Myers Squibb. Deal valued at $74 billion. Top Celgene executives stood to make millions once the deal closed, including Mark Alles who potentially would make an estimated $27.9 million
21
2020930Congressional ReportCongress publishes report on Revlimid https://oversightdemocrats.house.gov/imo/media/doc/Celgene%20BMS%20Staff%20Report%2009-30-2020.pdf "Mark Alles appears before House Oversight Committee, testifying on the high cost of prescription drugs. Pricing decisions reflected our commitment to patient access, the value of a medicine to patients and the health care system, the continuous effort to discover new medicines and new uses for existing medicines, and the need for financial flexibility. When it came time for questions, then-Rep. Katie Porter, D-Calif., quizzed Alles in rapid-fire style about Revlimid. Did the drug change as the price increased? Did it work faster? Were there fewer side effects? The drug was the same, Alles responded.
So, to recap here, Porter said. The drug didnt get any better. The cancer patients didnt get any better. You just got better at making money. You just refined your skills at price gouging."
